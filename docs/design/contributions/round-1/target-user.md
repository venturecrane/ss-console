# Target User Contribution - Design Brief Round 1

**Author:** Marcus (HVAC owner, Chandler AZ)
**Date:** 2026-04-26
**Design Maturity:** Full system (Plainspoken Sign Shop identity)

---

## Who I Am

My name's Marcus. I run a 14-person HVAC company out of Chandler — been doing it for twelve years. Started with one truck and me doing every call myself. Now I've got six techs, two install crews, a dispatcher position that's currently held together with duct tape and a 23-year-old who's trying her hardest, and a bookkeeper named Pat who handles QuickBooks on Tuesdays and Thursdays. I am not a software guy. I am not anti-technology. But I do not have time to learn new software just so some consultant can feel organized.

The reason I agreed to the assessment call — and I want to be honest about this — is that my scheduler quit in January and we've been bleeding since then. Jobs are getting dropped. Follow-ups aren't happening. I had a service call last month where the tech showed up and the customer had called to cancel two days earlier. Nobody caught it. I'm losing money and I know it, and I don't know how to fix the people problem, so maybe the answer is a process problem. That's what I'm hoping. I paid $250 to have that conversation, and I'm not sure yet if that was smart.

When I think about what success looks like — and this is the honest version, not the polished version — it's that three months from now I'm not the one who has to hold every single thing in my head. That someone could cover for me for two weeks and the company wouldn't fall apart. Right now it would. That's embarrassing to admit but it's true.

---

## My Environment

I check email on my phone. I check it between service calls, in parking lots, sometimes at red lights when I shouldn't. I read things on my phone but I sign things on my laptop — anything that requires me to actually read and commit, I'm doing that at the kitchen table after 7pm. My wife is usually watching something on the TV in the background. One of my kids will come in at least once. I'm tired. I don't want to puzzle anything out.

My phone is an iPhone 15. I don't have small hands but I don't have the giant-phone hands either. I can tap accurately if the targets are reasonable. I hate it when I have to pinch-zoom to read something. If I have to scroll sideways I'm closing the tab.

Laptop is a MacBook that's probably four years old. I use Chrome. I have somewhere between fifteen and thirty tabs open at any given time. This is not a habit I plan to change.

The apps I actually use and have learned because I had to: QuickBooks (Pat set it up), my bank's app, Apple Calendar, Google Maps, the Square reader app from when I was taking card payments at the counter before we moved everything to invoicing. I've heard of ServiceTitan. A couple other guys in the industry use it. It looks complicated and expensive. I looked at it for about ten minutes and closed the tab.

## First Impressions

Okay. Cream background, dark ink text, burnt orange button, no rounded corners. Arquivo typeface, heavy weight on the headings.

My first reaction — honest — is that it doesn't look like a website. Which is interesting, because most websites look the same now and I don't trust most of them. This looks like a letterhead. Like something you'd get from a contractor or a law office. Not a tech company trying to look friendly. There's nothing that says "sign up for a free trial" or "10x your results."

**That is actually a good sign.** I've been burned by slick-looking things before. The software that your lead-generation vendor sells you always looks incredible in the demo. This doesn't look like a demo. It looks like something a person made for a specific purpose.

The burnt orange is smart. It doesn't feel random. It has a little heat to it. It's not trying to be friendly. I don't need a logo to be my friend. I need it to not embarrass me when I show it to my wife and say "this is the company I hired."

The "Plainspoken Sign Shop" description — paint-job, not brochure — I wouldn't have said it that way myself but that's what I'm seeing. A painted sign on the front of a building. Not a Yelp ad. You can tell someone made a decision about what this should look like. That's reassuring. Indecision in how something looks makes me wonder what else is indecisive.

One concern: cream-on-cream. If the contrast isn't high enough on some elements I will not see them in direct sunlight in my truck. I've squinted at enough websites while the sun was coming through the windshield. The dark ink on cream should be fine if the contrast is actually good, but if any of the secondary text — the muted ink color — is too light, I'm not reading it.

## Emotional Reactions

### The portal invitation email arrives on my phone after the assessment call

I just had a two-hour conversation with this firm. I told them things about my business that I don't tell most people. I'm in a weird emotional state — part hopeful, part exposed. My phone buzzes. The email is from `team@smd.services`. Subject line references my company name.

**The subject line matters more than anything on the screen.** If it says "Your Portal Invitation" with no context, I'm mildly suspicious — spam filters and phishing have trained me to be. If it says "Marcus — your proposal is ready, [Company Name]" or something that makes it clear this is the follow-up to the call we just had, I'm clicking it. I might even click it before I get back to my truck.

There should be one button. One. "View Your Proposal." Not "View Your Proposal" and also "Learn More" and also some other link in the footer that I don't care about. I don't want to have to choose. If I have to make a decision about where to click I'm going to choose wrong or I'm going to close it.

What I do NOT want: anything that requires me to create an account. If the first screen after I tap that button says "Create your password," I am furious. I just paid money. I just gave you two hours. You don't get to add friction. I want to see my stuff.

### Magic-link login lands on my portal home

If the magic link works — which it better, because I've had magic links not work and it made me feel stupid even though it wasn't my fault — then I need to see my business name and my scope on the first screen. Not a welcome message. Not a tutorial. Not a progress bar asking me to "complete your profile." My information.

**The price and the "Review & Sign" button need to be on the screen without scrolling on my phone.** That's non-negotiable. If I have to scroll to find the price or the button, something has gone wrong in the layout. I should be able to look at this for ten seconds and understand what's here and what I need to do.

What I feel in this moment is cautious optimism. I want to feel like I made a good decision hiring these people. The screen either confirms that or it starts making me nervous. There is no neutral reaction. If the screen looks professional and clear, I feel good. If it looks like every generic project management tool that's ever been sold to small businesses — you know the look, the soft gray backgrounds, the friendly blue buttons, the little progress percentage — I feel like I got sold the same thing everyone else gets sold.

### Seeing my scope and price for the first time

This is the moment. Everything else is decoration.

The price is real money. I don't care what you tell me — $5,000 or $8,000 or $12,000 is real money for a company my size. I am not a startup with investor cash. That's a month of payroll for two of my guys. I need to feel like that price is connected to something real.

What makes me trust a price:

First, the problems listed on the screen have to match the conversation. **If I said "my scheduler problem is killing us" and the scope says "scheduling system optimization," that's close enough. If it says something generic that could apply to any business, I'm skeptical.** It needs to sound like you were listening to me specifically, not filling in a template.

Second, I don't want to see the hourly rate. I don't want to do the math. I don't want to wonder if the hours are padded. Show me the project price and what I'm getting. That's it.

Third, the payment structure — 50% now, 50% at the end — needs to be clear before I get to the signature page. Don't bury it. I want to know the exact dollar amounts, not just percentages. "$3,500 deposit due today. $3,500 at project completion." Say the numbers. Don't make me calculate.

What would make me trust it less: if the scope reads like it was written for a different industry. If the language is too formal or uses words I don't use in my business. If it sounds like they pasted in a template and swapped my company name in.

### The SignWell embed

Okay so I've signed things online before. DocuSign, mostly. My insurance renewal comes through DocuSign. It works fine. I know what it is.

If the SignWell embed is embedded well — meaning it looks like it belongs in the same visual register, not like a completely different application got dropped into the middle of the page — that's fine. What I don't want is to suddenly feel like I left the portal and ended up on some other company's website. That breaks my sense of "I know where I am."

**The worst thing that can happen here is visual whiplash.** If the portal is cream and dark ink and the SignWell window is white with blue buttons and a different typeface, my brain registers that as "you're not in Kansas anymore." That creates hesitation at exactly the moment when I need to be confident enough to sign. Hesitation is not good when you're asking someone to commit money.

On my phone I'd need the SignWell to be full-width. I'm not squinting to tap something in a half-width frame. If the scope summary above it collapses so I can actually see and use the signing interface, that's the right call. I'll read the scope first, then sign.

### The parking lot — items that need my decision

During the engagement, when the firm surfaces things they found but didn't address in the scope — okay. I understand why this happens. You start digging and you find more.

What I need to know in the parking lot is: what is this thing, why does it matter, and what do you want me to decide. Short. Plain English.

**What I do NOT want is to feel like this is a sales move.** If every item in the parking lot has a dollar sign attached and it feels like you're trying to upsell me, I'm going to get my guard up. I'm already in the middle of paying you. I need to believe that what's in the parking lot is genuinely "we found this and you should know about it," not "here's how we get another invoice out of you."

The decision needs to be a real three-way choice — address it now, defer it, or decide it's not relevant. I need to feel like "not relevant" is an acceptable answer. If the interface subtly pushes me toward "address it," I'll notice.

Being asked to make decisions is fine. **I make decisions all day.** What wastes my time is being asked to make decisions that were already made for me, or being asked for input on something where my input doesn't actually matter. If you're asking me to weigh in, the answer should be able to go any direction.

### The first invoice lands

The deposit invoice should look like an invoice from a professional firm, not a receipt from an app.

When I think of invoices that look right, I think of the invoices my commercial accounts send me. They're a little formal. They have a real company name, a date, an invoice number, the amount, the due date, and payment instructions. They don't have rounded corners and they don't have a friendly mascot waving at me.

**If the invoice looks like something I could have made in Canva in twenty minutes, I'm going to feel like I overpaid.** The visual register of the invoice should match the visual register of the portal — same typeface, same palette. Cream paper, ink, the amounts in a monospaced font so the numbers line up right. An invoice number that looks like an invoice number, not a UUID.

The "Pay Now" button should be obvious and it should tell me exactly what I'm about to pay. "$3,500 — Pay Now." Not just "Pay Now." I need the amount on the button. I'm not going to click a pay button to find out how much I owe.

### The engagement closes — final summary

If the work was good, the summary is almost secondary. The work speaks for itself.

But the summary matters for one reason: it's what I keep. It's the thing I'm going to look at in six months if my wife asks "what did we get out of that?" I need it to be a real account of what changed. Not marketing language. Not "we streamlined your operations" — what does that even mean. Specific. "We built a scheduling template and trained Sandra on it. Here's what's in the folder."

Did it feel like value? That depends on the work, not the summary screen. But the summary screen can kill the memory of good work if it's vague or reads like a report nobody wrote specifically for me.

## What Feels Right

**QuickBooks.** I trust it the way I trust an old Ford F-250. It's not pretty. Pat had to explain it to me three times. But every time I open it, I know where I am. The numbers are in columns. The columns mean something. It doesn't try to be anything other than what it is.

**My bank's app.** Boring. Functional. The balance is the first thing I see. Everything else is behind a tap. I don't need the bank app to be exciting.

**Apple Calendar.** I look at it fifty times a day. The information is dense but I can read it in under a second. There's no tutorial, no empty state with a friendly illustration of a cartoon calendar. It just shows me my schedule.

**Square.** When I was running card payments myself, Square just worked. The button was big. The confirmation was obvious. The receipt went to the customer. Nothing unnecessary. I never had to read instructions.

The portal should feel like these things. Not exciting. Reliable. Dense with the right information, not dense with decoration. The price should be as legible as my bank balance. The button should be as obvious as the Square pay button.

## What Would Turn Me Off

**Generic SaaS purple.** If I see that soft purple gradient that every "all-in-one platform for teams" uses, I'm out immediately. That color is the visual equivalent of "we want to appeal to everyone" and it appeals to no one.

**Stock photos.** If there's a photo of a diverse team smiling at a laptop on any page of this portal, I will immediately distrust everything else. That photo is a lie. It's a lie designed to make me feel comfortable, and the fact that it's a designed lie is what makes it untrustworthy.

**Dashboard-ification.** If the portal has a sidebar with seven icons, a notification bell, an avatar in the corner, and a "Getting Started" checklist, it's too much. I have one engagement. I'm not a power user. I don't need a navigation system designed for someone who uses this twelve hours a day.

**Cute empty states.** If I open the document library before any documents are uploaded and there's a little illustration of a folder with a face on it and text that says "No files yet! Check back soon 😊" — I'm judging the entire firm by that. It's not cute. It's juvenile. Just say "Documents will appear here as the project progresses" and move on.

**Rounded corners and drop shadows everywhere.** This sounds like a weird thing to have a strong opinion about but I do. Websites that have everything soft and floaty and layered feel like they're hiding something. Sharp edges feel like they're being honest with me. I know that doesn't make rational sense but that's how I feel.

**"Loading" that takes too long.** If I tap "Review & Sign" and nothing happens for three seconds, I'm tapping it again. And then I've tapped it twice and now I don't know what happened. Make the loading instant or show me something immediately that tells me it's working.

**Language I don't use.** "Handoff status." "Safety net period." "Scope disposition." I don't know what these mean in my business context. Write it in English. "We're finishing the work." "Your project is complete." "This item wasn't part of the original project — here's what to do with it."

## Navigation Expectations

One tap from the home screen:

- My scope (what I'm getting)
- My price and what I've paid / what I still owe
- The signature button if I haven't signed
- The current status of the project

That's it. Everything else — document library, parking lot, detailed milestone view — can be a tap deeper. I don't need it in my face. But if I'm looking for it, I should be able to find it in one tap from the dashboard.

I should never have to navigate through settings to find billing information. Never. Billing is not a setting. Billing is the whole transaction. It should be front and center until I've paid, and then it should be easy to find afterwards if I need a record.

If there's a "Help" link somewhere, fine. I'll probably never click it. But if the portal breaks — if a link doesn't work, if a page throws an error — I need a phone number or an email address that I can actually reach a human at. Not a contact form. Not a ticketing system. A way to reach the person I have a contract with.

---

## Make-or-Break Moments

**First authenticated screen.** This is it. If I click the magic link and land somewhere that makes sense immediately — my company name, my scope, my price, one button — I'm in. I've decided this was the right call. If I land somewhere confusing, something I have to figure out, I immediately feel the $250 assessment fee less justified and the project fee a lot less justified.

**Signing the quote.** The moment I go from "thinking about it" to "committed." If the signing flow feels like signing a real document — formal, serious, no friction that makes me second-guess — I'll do it and feel good about it. If it feels like I'm buying something in an app, I'll have a weird aftertaste.

**The deposit invoice.** When I pay the deposit, I want a confirmation that looks like something I'd print and put in a folder. This is business accounting. I need a record that doesn't look like I bought something on Etsy. If the invoice looks professional, I feel like I hired a professional firm. If it looks like an auto-generated receipt, I feel like I hired an app.

Those three moments. Get those right and everything else is manageable. Get any one of them wrong and I'm going to spend the rest of the engagement at low-grade skeptical.
